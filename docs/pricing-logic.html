§1 System Architecture

The Halchal Industries AI Pricing Engine is a real-time, demand-driven dynamic pricing system built specifically for the B2B drip irrigation pipe market in India. The engine computes a personalised delivered price for each customer based on their delivery state, the current agricultural season, real-time market demand, competitive landscape, and order volume.

Unlike static catalogue pricing, this system produces a price that changes with season, geography, and market conditions — making it a genuine dynamic pricing engine rather than a lookup table.

§2 Three-Layer Pipeline

Every price calculation flows through three sequential layers:

Layer 1
ML Demand Forecasting
Python · scikit-learn · Flask (port 5001)
Trained ensemble model predicts monthly demand (coils) for the given product, region, season, and market conditions. File: backend/ml/ml_api.py
Layer 2
Dynamic Price Calculation
Python · Flask (port 5001)
8-step algorithm converts predicted demand into a delivered price using demand factor, competitor positioning, logistics, and market adoption index. File: backend/ml/ml_api.py
Layer 3
B2B Order Pricing Engine
Node.js · Express (port 5000)
Applies volume-based bulk discounts and computes GST on the full order bill. File: backend/utils/pricingEngine.js

§3 Problem Formulation

Regression Task: Given product type, delivery region, agricultural season, calendar month, year, previous month's sales volume, and government subsidy status — predict the expected quantity of drip pipe coils sold per month (continuous target variable).

The ML model solves a supervised regression problem. The output (predicted demand) is not the final price — it is a demand signal that feeds the pricing algorithm in Layer 2 to adjust the price up or down relative to market conditions. This decouples demand forecasting from pricing strategy, allowing each to be tuned independently.

§5 Synthetic Data Generation Formula

Each record's demand is generated using a multiplicative factor model that captures the product-specific baseline, regional variation, seasonal variation, within-season monthly variation, year-on-year adoption growth, government subsidy effects, and stochastic market noise:

base = BASE_DEMAND[pipe] × ZONE_MULT[zone] × SEASON_MULT[season] × MONTH_PATTERN[month] × YEAR_GROWTH[year] if govt_subsidy == 1: base = base × 1.18 ← PMKSY subsidy boosts demand by 18% prev_sales = clip(base × Uniform(0.72, 1.28), 50, 1200) noise ~ Normal(0, base × 0.12) ← 12% std dev market noise qty_sold = max(30, base + noise) ← hard floor of 30 coils

The noise term (σ = 12% of base) models real-world stochasticity: weather shocks, credit cycle disruptions, competitor promotions, and localised demand spikes. The prev_sales feature is intentionally correlated with base demand (±28% random variation) to reflect the autocorrelation that exists in real monthly sales time series.

§7 Gradient Boosting Regressor

Library: sklearn.ensemble.GradientBoostingRegressor
Gradient Boosting builds an additive ensemble of shallow decision trees sequentially, where each tree corrects the residual errors of the previous ensemble. The model minimises Mean Squared Error using gradient descent in function space.

Mathematical Form

F_0(x) = initial prediction (mean of y) For m = 1 to M = 300: r_m = −∂L(y, F_{m-1}(x)) / ∂F_{m-1}(x) ← negative gradient (pseudo-residuals) h_m = decision tree fitted to r_m F_m(x) = F_{m-1}(x) + η · h_m(x) ← η = 0.05 (learning rate) Final prediction: F_300(x) = Σ η · h_m(x)

Hyperparameters

ParameterValuePurpose
n_estimators300Number of boosting stages — more trees = better fit, slower training
learning_rate0.05Shrinkage — smaller values reduce overfitting, require more trees
max_depth5Maximum depth of each individual tree
min_samples_leaf10Minimum samples per leaf — controls overfitting on small cells
subsample0.8Stochastic gradient boosting — each tree uses 80% of training data randomly sampled
random_state42Reproducibility seed

§8 Random Forest Regressor

Library: sklearn.ensemble.RandomForestRegressor
Random Forest builds multiple independent decision trees on bootstrap samples of the training data (bagging). Each split considers only a random subset of features (sqrt(n_features)). The final prediction is the arithmetic mean of all tree predictions.

Mathematical Form

For b = 1 to B = 200: D_b = bootstrap sample of training data (sampling with replacement) T_b = decision tree trained on D_b At each split: consider random subset of √8 ≈ 3 features Final prediction: ŷ = (1/B) Σ T_b(x) ← average over 200 trees

Hyperparameters

max_depth12Maximum tree depth

§9 Model Selection, Evaluation & Serialisation

Both models are trained and evaluated on the same 20% held-out test set. The model with the higher R² coefficient of determination is automatically selected and serialised to rf_demand_model.pkl (name is legacy; contains whichever model won).

§10 Categorical Encoding

The three categorical features (Pipe_Type, Region, Season) are ordinally encoded using sklearn.preprocessing.LabelEncoder, which assigns an integer (0, 1, 2, …) to each unique category. The fitted encoders are serialised to label_encoders.pkl and reloaded at inference time to guarantee consistent mappings between training and production.

§11–18 Dynamic Price Calculation — 8-Step Algorithm

Once the ML model predicts demand, the Flask API applies an 8-step deterministic algorithm to compute the final delivered price. All steps are explicit, auditable, and interpretable — making the system explainable to domain experts and regulators.

§11 Step 1 — ML Demand Prediction

predicted_demand = model.predict([pipe_enc, region_enc, season_enc, month, year, base_price, prev_month_sales, govt_subsidy])

At inference time, prev_month_sales is fetched dynamically from MongoDB Atlas by aggregating all orders for the same product category in the last 30 days:

// Node.js — live prev_month_sales from real order history const minId = new ObjectId(floor(30daysAgo / 1000).toString(16) + "0000000000000000"); const salesAgg = await Order.aggregate([ { $match: { _id: { $gte: minId }, pipe_type: { $regex: category } } }, { $group: { _id: null, total: { $sum: "$quantity" } } } ]); const prevMonthSales = salesAgg[0]?.total ?? 450; // fallback if no orders yet

§12 Step 2 — Demand Factor

Converts predicted demand into a bounded multiplicative pricing multiplier:

demand_ratio = predicted_demand / avg_demand demand_factor = 1 + 0.15 × (demand_ratio − 1) demand_factor = clamp(demand_factor, 0.90, 1.10)
Scenariodemand_ratiodemand_factorPrice effect
Demand 33% above average1.331.05Price ceiling reached (+5%)
Demand at average1.001.00No change
Demand 33% below average0.670.95Price floor reached (−5%)

The 0.15 sensitivity coefficient ensures only 15% of demand deviation translates to price movement, preventing volatile price swings from noisy demand signals. avg_demand is loaded from avg_demand.json (historical mean per pipe type computed at training time).

§14 Step 4 — Ex-Factory Price with Smoothing

raw_price = base_price × demand_factor × competitor_factor smoothed_price = 0.70 × base_price + 0.30 × raw_price ex_factory = clamp(smoothed_price, base_price × 0.90, base_price × 1.10)

70/30 exponential-like smoothing: Assigns 70% weight to the stable, known base price and only 30% to the ML-computed dynamic component. This prevents excessive price volatility while still allowing meaningful market-driven adjustment — analogous to a weighted moving average in time-series smoothing.

±10% safety cap: The ex-factory price is hard-clamped to within 10% of the manufacturer's base price, regardless of demand signals. This protects against model overfitting or extreme demand predictions producing unrealistic prices.

Models price elasticity based on each state's drip irrigation adoption maturity index. §17 Steps 7 & 8 — Final Delivered Price & Rounding

final_price = ex_factory × logistics_factor × market_factor ← Step 7 final_price = round(final_price / 10) × 10 ← Step 8 (round to ₹10)

Rounding to the nearest ₹10 is standard B2B pricing practice in India — it eliminates psychological friction from odd values (e.g. ₹1,237 → ₹1,240) and is consistent with how drip pipe dealers quote prices in the Maharashtra market.

§18 State → Agricultural Zone Mapping

The ML model was trained exclusively on 8 Maharashtra agricultural zones. To extend pricing coverage to all 36 Indian states and Union Territories without retraining, every Indian state is mapped to its agriculturally closest Maharashtra zone. The ML model then uses that zone as the Region input feature.

Design rationale: This decoupling allows pan-India pricing without expanding the training dataset. The ML model handles demand prediction using the zone's agricultural profile. State-specific variation beyond zone similarity is captured by the separate logistics and market factors applied after the ML output.

§19 Bulk Discount Tiers

After the ML-delivered price is computed (Layer 2), the B2B order pricing engine (Layer 3) applies volume-based bulk discounts that reflect standard dealer/distributor economics in the Maharashtra drip irrigation market.

discountFactor: quantity ≥ 500 coils → 0.92 (8% discount) quantity ≥ 100 coils → 0.94 (6% discount) quantity ≥ 50 coils → 0.96 (4% discount) quantity ≥ 10 coils → 0.98 (2% discount) quantity ≥ 5 coils → 0.99 (1% discount) quantity < 5 coils → 1.00 (no discount) pricePerCoil = round((aiPrice × discountFactor) / 10) × 10
Business rationale: Large B2B buyers (dealers, distributors, irrigation contractors) ordering 100+ coils receive a 6% discount. Orders of 500+ coils (large-scale canal/project irrigation) receive the maximum 8% discount. This incentivises volume purchasing while maintaining margin on small retail orders.

§21 Complete End-to-End Formula Chain

Full Pricing Pipeline — Steps 1–9

STEP 1 predicted_demand = ML_Model(pipe, zone, season, month, year, prev_sales, subsidy) ← ensemble model output STEP 2 demand_ratio = predicted_demand / avg_demand demand_factor = clamp(1 + 0.15 × (demand_ratio − 1), 0.90, 1.10) STEP 3 price_diff_ratio = (competitor_price − base_price) / base_price competitor_factor = f(demand_factor, price_diff_ratio) STEP 4 raw_price = base_price × demand_factor × competitor_factor smoothed_price = 0.70 × base_price + 0.30 × raw_price ex_factory = clamp(smoothed_price, base × 0.90, base × 1.10) STEP 5 logistics_factor = STATE_LOGISTICS_FACTOR[state] STEP 6 market_factor = STATE_MARKET_FACTOR[state] STEP 7 final_price = ex_factory × logistics_factor × market_factor STEP 8 final_price = round(final_price / 10) × 10 ← ML API output (₹/coil) STEP 9 pricePerCoil = round((final_price × discountFactor) / 10) × 10 totalExGST = pricePerCoil × quantity totalGST = totalExGST × 0.12 totalWithGST = totalExGST + totalGST ← Final invoice amount
