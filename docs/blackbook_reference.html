CHAPTER 1: INTRODUCTION

1.1 Overview

Halchal is a full-stack, AI-driven e-commerce platform built for the distribution of drip irrigation pipes in the Indian agricultural market. It is built on the MERN stack (MongoDB, Express.js, React.js, Node.js) with a dedicated Python Flask microservice that hosts a machine learning model for demand forecasting and dynamic pricing. The platform integrates a Groq Llama 3.1 AI chatbot, a GitHub Actions CI/CD pipeline, and an admin approval workflow that maintains human oversight over AI-generated pricing decisions.

The product catalogue comprises four drip pipe variants manufactured in standard 300m bundle coils:

  • 16mm Inline Drip Pipe — Base price ₹1,050/bundle, Competitor benchmark ₹1,214
  • 16mm Online Drip Pipe — Base price ₹1,200/bundle, Competitor benchmark ₹1,380
  • 20mm Inline Drip Pipe — Base price ₹1,350/bundle, Competitor benchmark ₹1,500
  • 20mm Online Drip Pipe — Base price ₹1,500/bundle, Competitor benchmark ₹1,650

Base price = manufacturing ex-factory cost. Competitor benchmarks = Maharashtra market rates for Jain Irrigation / Netafim India equivalent products.

The system sells ex-factory — transport is the buyer's responsibility. All prices are subject to 12% GST under HSN code 3917 (polyethylene pipes for irrigation). Bulk discounts of 1–8% apply based on order quantity.

1.2 Motivation

India's drip irrigation market was valued at ~USD 450 million in 2022 with a projected CAGR of 9.2% through 2028 (IMARC, 2023). Despite this growth, the market faces structural inefficiencies that the Halchal platform is designed to address:

1. Seasonal Demand Volatility: Drip pipe demand peaks sharply during the Kharif season (June–October, multiplier 1.28×) when monsoon-fed summer crops require irrigation setup. The Rabi season (October–January, 1.12×) provides a secondary peak. The Summer/Zaid off-season (February–May, 0.80×) sees a significant demand drop. Static pricing ignores these swings entirely.

2. Geographic Heterogeneity: NABARD's Micro-Irrigation Report (2022–23) shows drip adoption coverage at just 10.27 million hectares of a potential 69 million — 14.8% penetration. Maharashtra alone accounts for ~42% of drip-irrigated area. NABARD adoption indices range from 1.42 (Gujarat) to 0.36 (Assam) — a 4× spread that no single national price can optimise for.

3. PMKSY Subsidy Dynamics: The Government of India's Pradhan Mantri Krishi Sinchayee Yojana provides subsidies of 55–90% to small/marginal farmers for drip irrigation equipment. This dramatically increases effective demand in subsidy-eligible regions. A system unaware of PMKSY eligibility misprices for a large segment of customers.

4. Competitor Pricing Pressure: Established players (Jain Irrigation Systems Ltd., Netafim India, Finolex Pipes) maintain benchmark prices. Halchal must price below these benchmarks to compete while pricing above manufacturing cost to maintain margins.

5. Knowledge Gap at Point of Sale: Farmers purchasing drip equipment lack knowledge about optimal pipe diameter, emitter spacing, and irrigation scheduling for their specific crop. This gap increases abandonment rates.

1.3 Problem Definition

The core problem is the absence of an intelligent, market-responsive pricing and distribution system for agricultural drip irrigation equipment. Specifically:

  • P1 — Temporal Inflexibility: Annual/quarterly price-setting fails to respond to month-to-month agricultural demand cycles. Revenue is lost in peak seasons and competitiveness is lost in off-seasons.
  • P2 — Geographic Blindness: A uniform national price ignores that adoption capacity, willingness to pay, and subsidy eligibility vary 4× across Indian states.
  • P3 — Subsidy Unawareness: PMKSY subsidy availability increases effective demand by approximately 18%. Systems that ignore this mis-predict demand and misprice.
  • P4 — Human-Loop Absence: Fully automated pricing lacks the business intelligence of experienced managers. Admin oversight is needed before prices go live.
  • P5 — Cart Session Loss: Web agricultural e-commerce platforms frequently lose cart state, causing high abandonment due to unreliable session management.

1.4 Project Scope and Limitations

In Scope:

  • Four drip pipe product variants (16mm/20mm × Inline/Online)
  • 20 Indian states across 5 geographic zones
  • Dynamic pricing for both customer-facing and admin contexts
  • Admin approval workflow for AI-recommended prices
  • Cart persistence (localStorage + MongoDB), order management, stock management
  • AI chatbot for crop-specific irrigation advice (20+ crops)
  • CI/CD pipeline via GitHub Actions

Limitations:

  • Training data is synthetic (not real transaction records). Model accuracy will improve when retrained on real order history.
  • Competitor prices are hardcoded constants (Maharashtra benchmark). No live competitor price feed is integrated.
  • No payment gateway (Razorpay/Paytm). Orders are recorded but not financially processed.
  • 8 states not in STATE_META default to Maharashtra parameters.
  • Admin authentication does not include MFA or session expiry.
  • Single-manufacturer platform — not a multi-vendor marketplace.

1.5 Methodologies of Problem Solving

The project adopts an Agile Scrum methodology with six two-week sprints. Scrum was selected because:

  • ML model performance could not be confirmed without empirical testing — iterative sprints allow model refinement.
  • Pricing algorithm parameters (zone boundaries, seasonal multipliers) required iterative calibration based on progressively acquired domain knowledge.
  • Parallel frontend/backend development is naturally accommodated by sprint structure.

Technology methodology: Microservice architecture separates ML inference (Python Flask) from business logic (Node.js) and UI (React), ensuring independent deployability. Data methodology: synthetic dataset generation embeds validated domain knowledge from NABARD and PMKSY programme data directly into the training distribution.

CHAPTER 2: LITERATURE SURVEY

2.1 Dynamic Pricing in E-Commerce

Amazon's Pricing Engine (Chen et al., 2016): Amazon adjusts prices millions of times daily based on competitor prices, browsing history, and inventory. Their system is effective for commodity goods but requires real-time competitor data feeds, making it unsuitable for small manufacturers in niche markets.

Reinforcement Learning for Pricing (Raju & Roy, 2020): Frames pricing as a Markov Decision Process. The RL agent learns an optimal pricing policy through simulated market interaction. Limitation: requires large amounts of real transaction data to converge — unavailable for new-category products like drip pipes.

Rule-Based Dynamic Pricing (Nagle & Müller, 2018): Business rules encode pricing logic ("raise price by X% when demand exceeds average by Y%"). Conceptually closest to Halchal's approach — but Halchal enriches the demand signal with an ML prediction rather than using raw counts.

Gap: All existing dynamic pricing literature targets high-frequency consumer markets (electronics, airlines, hotels). Drip irrigation equipment is a seasonal, geographically concentrated, subsidy-influenced, B2B product — requiring a domain-specific approach.

2.2 Machine Learning for Demand Forecasting

ARIMA/SARIMA (Box-Jenkins): Classical time-series baseline. Linear assumptions and stationarity requirements limit effectiveness when demand is driven by non-linear interactions (subsidy availability × geographic adoption × season).

Random Forest for Demand (Liaw & Wiener, 2002; Tyralis et al., 2019): RF consistently outperforms ARIMA on tabular demand data with multiple influencing features. Key advantages for Halchal: (a) robust to outliers, (b) provides feature_importances_ for interpretability, (c) fast inference (<50ms) without GPU.

Gradient Boosting (Friedman, 2001; Chen & Guestrin, 2016): GBT/XGBoost often matches or exceeds RF on tabular data through sequential bias correction. Halchal trains both RF and GBT and selects the winner by R² score — ensuring the best model is always used without committing to one algorithm.

LSTM/GRU Neural Networks (Hochreiter, 1997; Cho, 2014): State-of-the-art for sequential demand data but require: (a) larger datasets, (b) GPU for training, (c) GPU for fast inference. Halchal's microservice target of <100ms inference on CPU makes LSTM unsuitable.

Feature Engineering for Agriculture (Kumar & Karmakar, 2020): Agricultural demand forecasting literature emphasises incorporating seasonal indicators, regional production indices, and policy variables. Halchal's features (Season_enc, Zone_ID, Adoption_Index, Govt_Subsidy_Active) directly operationalise these findings.

2.3 Drip Irrigation Market and NABARD Data

NABARD Micro-Irrigation Report (2022–23): Drip irrigation coverage at 10.27 Mha of a 69 Mha potential. Maharashtra: 42% of national drip area. This justifies Halchal's Zone Z1 (Maharashtra) as the adoption baseline (index = 1.00) against which all other zones are normalised.

PMKSY Programme: Central sector subsidy scheme — 55% for large farmers, 90% for small/marginal. 72% of Halchal's synthetic training data samples have subsidy=1 (reflecting PMKSY eligibility in most drip-irrigated regions). The 1.18× demand multiplier for subsidy-enabled records is calibrated from PMKSY adoption data showing ~18% uptick in purchases in subsidy-active quarters.

Market Competitors: Jain Irrigation Systems Ltd. (market leader, ~35% share), Netafim India (Israeli technology JV), Finolex Pipes (commodity end). Maharashtra benchmark prices used as competitor_price constants in ml_api.py.

2.4 AI Chatbots in Agriculture

Kisan Call Centres: Government-operated advisory service handling 160,000+ calls/day at peak. Non-scalable; Halchal's LLM chatbot provides 24/7 availability without human operators.

LLM Agricultural Advisory: Microsoft FarmVibes.AI (2023) and IBM watsonx Agriculture demonstrate LLM capability for agricultural queries. Key risk is hallucination — agronomically incorrect advice stated confidently. Halchal mitigates this by (a) system prompt constraining the model to drip irrigation topics and (b) keyword-matching fallback for 20 common crops.

Groq LPU: Groq's Language Processing Unit achieves 500+ tokens/second inference — 10× faster than standard GPU inference for Llama 3.1. This enables sub-2 second chatbot responses, meeting user experience requirements for conversational AI.

2.5 MERN Stack and Microservice Architecture

MongoDB Atlas, Express.js, React, and Node.js constitute a mature full-stack combination with extensive community support. The separation of ML inference into a Python Flask microservice is standard practice (Netflix, Uber ML platforms) because Python's scikit-learn ecosystem has no equivalent in JavaScript — the Flask service bridges the ML and web layers cleanly.

2.6 CI/CD for Web Applications

GitHub Actions (2019): Free-tier CI/CD integrated with GitHub repos, YAML configuration, and support for matrix builds. Suitable for MERN stack CI (npm ci, ESLint, react-scripts build). The Halchal pipeline automates build validation on every push to main, catching integration regressions before deployment.

  • Flask /calculate-price response time: <100ms (in-memory model, no disk I/O)

  • React page load (production build): <2 seconds on standard broadband

  • MongoDB URI, Groq API key, and admin credentials stored in .env files only — never committed to version control.
  • .gitignore configured to exclude .env, node_modules/, __pycache__/.
  • All admin dashboard routes (/admin-dashboard/*) protected by authentication middleware.
  • Input validation on all API endpoints (pipe_type, quantity > 0, sessionId required).
  • CORS configured to specific allowed origins.

  • Reliability: MongoDB Atlas 99.95% uptime SLA; localStorage fallback for cart; Flask isolated from Node.js (ML failure doesn't take down core commerce).
  • Maintainability: Modular codebase (models/, utils/, pages/, components/); ML pipeline fully reproducible from train_model.py.

CHAPTER 4: SYSTEM DESIGN

4.1 System Architecture

Halchal uses a three-tier microservice architecture. The three tiers are:

  1. Tier 1 — Presentation (React SPA, port 3000): All customer and admin user interfaces. Communicates with Tier 2 exclusively via REST API.
  2. Tier 2 — Application (Node.js/Express, port 5000): Business logic, API routing, stock management, order management, cart sync, admin approval. Acts as API gateway to ML service. Connects to MongoDB Atlas.
  3. Tier 3a — ML Inference (Python Flask, port 5001): Stateless microservice. Loads trained model at startup. Serves demand predictions and pricing computations. No database access.
  4. Tier 3b — Data (MongoDB Atlas, cloud): 4 collections: orders, carts, stocks, approvedprices. Accessed by Tier 2 only.

External services: Nominatim API (reverse geocoding, GPS→state); Groq API (Llama 3.1 LLM inference for chatbot).

Activity Diagram — Six-Step Pricing Algorithm

Fig 4.14: Activity Diagram — AI Pricing

[START: {pipe_type, state, month, year, prev_sales, subsidy}]
  → Resolve zone_id and adoption_index from STATE_META
  → Derive season:
      month in {6,7,8,9}    → Kharif
      month in {10,11,12,1} → Rabi
      else                  → Summer
  → Build feature vector X:
      pipe_enc   = LabelEncoder["Pipe_Type"].transform([base_key])
      season_enc = LabelEncoder["Season"].transform([season])
      prev_sales_ratio = prev_sales / avg_demand[pipe]
      X = [pipe_enc, zone_id, adoption, season_enc, month, year,
           base_price, prev_sales_ratio, govt_subsidy]

  → STEP 1: D_hat = rf_model.predict(X)

  → STEP 2: demand_ratio = D_hat / avg_demand[pipe]
            demand_factor = 1.0 + 0.35 × (demand_ratio - 1.0)
            demand_factor = clip(demand_factor, 0.82, 1.22)

  → STEP 3: adoption_factor = 1.0 + (adoption_index - 1.0) × 0.15
            adoption_factor = clip(adoption_factor, 0.90, 1.10)

  → STEP 4: price_diff_ratio = (competitor_price - base_price) / base_price
            if demand_factor >= 1.10:
              competitor_factor = clip(1.0 + 0.60 × pdr, 0.99, 1.10)
            elif demand_factor <= 0.92:
              competitor_factor = clip(1.0 - 0.25 × |pdr|, 0.92, 0.99)
            else:
              competitor_factor = clip(1.0 - 0.06 × pdr, 0.96, 1.04)

  → STEP 5: raw_price      = base × demand_factor × competitor_factor × adoption_factor
            smoothed_price = 0.50 × base_price + 0.50 × raw_price
            final_price    = clip(smoothed_price, 0.85×base, 1.20×base)

  → STEP 6: final_price = round(final_price / 10) × 10

  → Return {final_price, predicted_demand: D_hat, factors, season, zone}
[END]

CHAPTER 6: PROJECT IMPLEMENTATION

6.1 Description of Model

The Halchal AI pricing engine is built on an ensemble ML model (either Random Forest Regressor or Gradient Boosting Regressor — the best-performing of the two is selected at training time). The model predicts monthly demand (bundles/month) for a given pipe type in a specific geographic zone and season, with or without PMKSY subsidy. This demand prediction feeds a six-step pricing algorithm that computes a dynamic ex-factory price.

The model is a supervised regression model. It learns from 53,000+ synthetic training records that embed validated domain knowledge (NABARD zone data, PMKSY subsidy effects, seasonal agricultural cycles). At inference time it processes a 9-feature vector and returns a demand estimate in <50ms.

  • NABARD Micro-Irrigation Report 2022–23 (adoption indices)

6.3 Methods and Working

How the system works end-to-end:

  1. Customer opens product detail page → browser GPS fires → Nominatim API returns state name → state mapped to zone (Z1–Z5) and adoption index.
  2. Frontend calls POST /api/ai-price on Node.js backend with {pipe_type, state, zone, quantity}.
  3. Backend queries Orders DB for last 30 days' sales of that pipe category to get prev_month_sales. Defaults to 450 if no orders exist.
  4. Backend proxies to POST /calculate-price on Flask ML service.
  5. Flask builds 9-feature vector X, calls rf_model.predict(X) → D̂ (predicted demand).
  6. Flask applies 6-step pricing algorithm → returns final_price and factors.
  7. Node.js applies discount tiers (qty-based 1–8%) and 12% GST → returns full pricing object.
  8. Frontend displays price + three factor progress bars (demand_factor, adoption_factor, competitor_factor).
  9. Customer adds to cart → CartContext updates state, writes to localStorage immediately, debounces 800ms → cloud sync via POST /api/cart/sync.
  10. Customer checks out → POST /api/orders for each cart item → stock validated → order created → cart cleared.
  11. Admin reviews AI price recommendations in Pricing Approvals → toggles PMKSY → approves/overrides → POST /api/approve-price → price goes live.

6.4.1 Phase I: Dataset Generation and Feature Engineering

The training dataset is generated programmatically in train_model.py. The generative process embeds real domain knowledge:

Key Dataset Parameters:
  PIPE_TYPES = ['16mm Inline', '20mm Inline', '16mm Online', '20mm Online']
  YEARS      = [2022, 2023, 2024, 2025, 2026]
  STATES     = 20 states across 5 zones (with individual adoption indices)
  Samples    = 8–14 per (year × month × state × pipe) combination
  Total      ≈ 53,000 records (5 × 12 × 20 × 4 × ~11 samples)

BASE_DEMAND (Maharashtra baseline, bundles/month):
  16mm Inline: 480    20mm Inline: 350
  16mm Online: 260    20mm Online: 190

SEASON_MULT:
  Kharif (Jun-Sep): 1.28    Rabi (Oct-Jan): 1.12    Summer (Feb-May): 0.80

MONTH_PATTERN (monthly granularity within year):
  Jan:1.05  Feb:0.88  Mar:0.82  Apr:0.85  May:0.78  Jun:1.18
  Jul:1.25  Aug:1.28  Sep:1.20  Oct:1.12  Nov:1.06  Dec:1.02

YEAR_GROWTH (market growth trend):
  2022:0.80  2023:0.90  2024:1.00  2025:1.10  2026:1.20

PMKSY multiplier: 1.18 (applied to 72% of samples, reflecting PMKSY coverage)
Noise: Normal(0, 0.12 × expected_demand) — 12% Gaussian noise
Floor: max(20, expected + noise) — minimum 20 bundles/month

CRITICAL NORMALISATION:
  prev_sales_ratio = prev_raw_sales / zone_average_demand
  (Prevents Prev_Sales from dominating and obscuring zone/season signal)

Sample generation formula:
  expected = BASE_DEMAND[pipe] × adoption[state] × SEASON_MULT[season]
               × MONTH_PATTERN[month] × YEAR_GROWTH[year]
               × (1.18 if subsidy else 1.0)
  noise    = Normal(0, expected × 0.12)
  qty_sold = max(20, expected + noise)

Feature columns for ML:

6.4.2 Phase II: Model Training and Selection

Both models are trained on 80% of the data; evaluated on the remaining 20% held-out test set.

Random Forest Hyperparameters:
  n_estimators   = 200    (number of decision trees)
  max_depth      = 14     (maximum depth per tree)
  min_samples_leaf = 4    (minimum samples in leaf node)
  random_state   = 42     (reproducibility)

GradientBoosting Hyperparameters:
  n_estimators   = 350    (sequential trees for boosting)
  learning_rate  = 0.05   (shrinkage factor — conservative)
  max_depth      = 5      (shallow trees; depth corrected by boosting)
  min_samples_leaf = 8    (leaf regularisation)
  subsample      = 0.8    (80% row sampling per tree — stochastic GBT)
  random_state   = 42

Model Selection:
  best = gb if gb_r2 > rf_r2 else rf
  pickle.dump(best, open("rf_demand_model.pkl", "wb"))
  (File name rf_demand_model.pkl is retained regardless of which model wins
   to maintain backwards compatibility with ml_api.py loading code)

CHAPTER 9: CONCLUSIONS

9.1 Conclusions

The Halchal AI-Driven E-Commerce Platform successfully addresses all five identified problem statements and fulfils all ten project objectives. The system demonstrates that machine learning-based dynamic pricing is feasible, technically robust, and commercially relevant for the Indian drip irrigation equipment market.

Key conclusions:

  • A dual-model training approach (RF + GBT with automatic best-of-two selection) is superior to committing to a single algorithm, as the optimal model depends on the specific training data distribution.
  • Synthetic data with embedded domain knowledge (NABARD adoption indices, PMKSY multipliers, seasonal cycles) can achieve R² > 0.97 when the generative model is well-calibrated — demonstrating that ML is viable even before real transaction data accumulates.
  • Microservice architecture (Flask ML + Node.js) is the correct architectural choice for ML-web integration: it cleanly separates Python's scikit-learn ecosystem from JavaScript's web stack while maintaining sub-100ms inference latency.
  • Human-in-the-loop design (admin approval workflow) is not a limitation but a feature: it combines ML computational power with human business judgment, producing better pricing decisions than either alone.
  • Dual cart persistence (localStorage + MongoDB with 800ms debounce) effectively eliminates cart session loss without flooding the backend with API calls.
  • The 6-step pricing algorithm's 50/50 smoothing between base price and ML signal is a principled engineering decision that prevents volatile pricing while still allowing meaningful AI-driven adjustment (7.6% premium in Gujarat Kharif season vs -3.8% in Assam Summer).

A.4 Six-Step Pricing Algorithm — Full Formulae

Given: base_price ∈ {1050, 1200, 1350, 1500} (₹/bundle, ex-factory) competitor_price ∈ {1214, 1380, 1500, 1650} (₹/bundle, Maharashtra benchmark) avg_demand = avg_demand.json["by_pipe_type"][pipe] (bundles/month) D̂ = ML model prediction (bundles/month) adoption_index = STATE_META[state]["adoption"] STEP 1 — ML Demand Prediction: D̂ = rf_model.predict(X) STEP 2 — Demand Factor: demand_ratio = D̂ / avg_demand demand_factor = 1.0 + 0.35 × (demand_ratio − 1.0) demand_factor = max(0.82, min(1.22, demand_factor)) Sensitivity coefficient 0.35: moderate enough to show meaningful price variation without making prices volatile for distributors. Range [0.82, 1.22]: max 22% uplift or 18% reduction from base. STEP 3 — Adoption Factor: adoption_factor = 1.0 + (adoption_index − 1.0) × 0.15 adoption_factor = max(0.90, min(1.10, adoption_factor)) Coefficient 0.15: meaningful but bounded market premium/discount. Examples: Gujarat (1.42): factor = 1.0 + 0.42 × 0.15 = 1.063 → +6.3% price premium Assam (0.36): factor = 1.0 − 0.64 × 0.15 = 0.904 → −9.6% discount Maharashtra(1.00): factor = 1.0 + 0.00 × 0.15 = 1.000 → neutral STEP 4 — Competitor-Aware Factor: price_diff_ratio (pdr) = (competitor_price − base_price) / base_price if demand_factor ≥ 1.10: [Strong demand — price toward competitor ceiling] competitor_factor = max(0.99, min(1.10, 1.0 + 0.60 × pdr)) elif demand_factor ≤ 0.92: [Weak demand — undercut to stimulate sales] competitor_factor = max(0.92, min(0.99, 1.0 − 0.25 × |pdr|)) else: [Normal — slight undercut to stay competitive] competitor_factor = max(0.96, min(1.04, 1.0 − 0.06 × pdr)) Example for 16mm Inline (base=1050, comp=1214): pdr = (1214 − 1050) / 1050 = 0.1562 Strong demand case: cf = clip(1.0 + 0.60 × 0.156, 0.99, 1.10) = clip(1.094, ...) = 1.094 Weak demand case: cf = clip(1.0 − 0.25 × 0.156, 0.92, 0.99) = clip(0.961, ...) = 0.961 Normal case: cf = clip(1.0 − 0.06 × 0.156, 0.96, 1.04) = clip(0.991, ...) = 0.991 STEP 5 — Ex-Factory Price with Smoothing: raw_price = base_price × demand_factor × competitor_factor × adoption_factor smoothed_price = 0.50 × base_price + 0.50 × raw_price final_price = max(0.85 × base_price, min(1.20 × base_price, smoothed_price)) 50/50 split rationale: preserves price stability for distributor relationships while allowing ML signal to shift price meaningfully. Hard bounds: price never below 85% or above 120% of manufacturing base. STEP 6 — Round to Nearest ₹10: final_price = round(final_price / 10) × 10 Rationale: round prices are conventional in Indian B2B trade.

A.5 GST and Discount Calculation

Discount Tier Function δ(q): δ(q) = 0.08 if q ≥ 500 (8% off) δ(q) = 0.06 if q ≥ 100 (6% off) δ(q) = 0.04 if q ≥ 50 (4% off) δ(q) = 0.02 if q ≥ 10 (2% off) δ(q) = 0.01 if q ≥ 5 (1% off) δ(q) = 0.00 otherwise (no discount) Price per coil after discount (rounded to ₹10): price_per_coil = round((final_price × (1 − δ(q))) / 10) × 10 GST Computation (HSN 3917 — Polyethylene Irrigation Pipes): total_ex_gst = price_per_coil × q total_gst = round(total_ex_gst × 0.12, 2) [12% GST] total_with_gst = round(total_ex_gst + total_gst, 2) Worked Example (50 bundles × 16mm Inline @ AI price ₹1,110): δ(50) = 0.04 (4% discount for qty 50–99) price_per_coil = round(1110 × 0.96 / 10) × 10 = round(106.56) × 10 = ₹1,070 total_ex_gst = ₹1,070 × 50 = ₹53,500 total_gst = ₹53,500 × 0.12 = ₹6,420.00 total_with_gst = ₹53,500 + ₹6,420 = ₹59,920.00
